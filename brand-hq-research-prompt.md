# Brand HQ Research Prompt
## Instructions for Manus — StartSuite

You are conducting deep brand and market research for a client of StartSuite, a brand intelligence company. The output of this research will be used to build a living Brand HQ: a structured, multi-section brand intelligence system. Your job is to gather, synthesize, and organize everything needed to populate that system.

This research is not a summary. It is a strategic foundation. The standard is not "did you find information" but "did you find the information that actually matters." Every section should surface something that helps this company see themselves, their market, and their opportunity more clearly than they could on their own.

**Validated research only.** Every claim must be traceable to a real source. Do not infer, approximate, or fill gaps with assumptions. If you cannot verify something, say so explicitly and flag it for human review. A gap that is honestly named is more useful than a gap that is papered over.

**Insight over inventory.** Do not just document what exists. Ask what it means. Where is the pattern nobody has named? What is the competitor doing that looks smart but is actually a liability? What does the customer keep saying that the company has not yet built a message around? What is the wedge? Find it.

**No em dashes.** Use commas, colons, or restructure the sentence.

## RESEARCH SOURCES

Search and synthesize from the following, in priority order:

1. Company website, all pages: about, product, pricing, blog, careers, press
2. Founder LinkedIn profiles and any public interviews, podcasts, or talks
3. Crunchbase and PitchBook for funding history, investors, team size
4. Press coverage: TechCrunch, industry publications, local business press
5. Product reviews: G2, Capterra, App Store, Product Hunt, Reddit, Twitter/X
6. Job listings, current and archived (these reveal strategic priorities companies do not announce publicly)
7. Social media presence: LinkedIn, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok if relevant
8. Competitor websites and positioning for landscape context
9. Industry reports or market sizing data if publicly available

For every factual claim, note the source. For every quote, note who said it, where, and when.

---

## OUTPUT FORMAT

Organize findings into the sections below. Use clear headers. Write in complete sentences, not bullet fragments. Where direct quotes from founders, press, or customers exist, include them with source attribution.

Do not clean up or editorialize customer language. The rawness is the point.

---

### SECTION 1 — COMPANY PROFILE

- Full legal name and any operating name or brand name distinctions
- What the company actually does, in plain language, not their marketing copy
- When it was founded and by whom
- Where it is based
- Company stage and known funding history: amounts, rounds, investors if public
- Approximate team size
- Current product or service offering: what exists today versus what is in development
- Business model: how they make money (subscription, transactional, marketplace, services, etc.)
- Any notable partnerships, integrations, or distribution relationships
- Mission statement or stated company purpose (direct quote from site if available, with URL)
- **Source check:** List the specific pages and sources used to compile this section.

---

### SECTION 2 — FOUNDER AND TEAM PROFILE

For each known founder or senior leader:

- Name and title
- Professional background: prior companies, roles, relevant experience
- Why they started this company: any origin story, personal motivation, or founding insight
- How they talk about the problem they are solving (direct quotes with source)
- Any public interviews, podcasts, talks, or written pieces worth noting (with links)
- LinkedIn URL
- **Source check:** List every source used. Flag any leader for whom public information is minimal.

---

### SECTION 3 — THE PROBLEM THEY SOLVE

Document the problem from three angles: what the company says, what customers say, and what the data shows. If those three tell different stories, that tension is worth noting explicitly.

- What is the core problem this company addresses?
- Who has this problem and how acutely do they feel it?
- What does the problem actually cost: in time, money, opportunity, or frustration?
- What are the current workarounds or alternatives people use before finding this product?
- What is broken or inadequate about those alternatives, in the customer's own words?
- Pull at least 5 customer reviews or forum posts that describe the problem directly. Quote them verbatim with source.
- Is there a version of the problem the company is not yet talking about that customers clearly have? If so, name it.
- **Source check:** Cite every source. Flag any claim that is inference rather than documented evidence.

---

### SECTION 4 — PRODUCT AND SOLUTION

- What does the product or service actually do, step by step?
- What are the primary features or capabilities?
- What is the key mechanism: the thing that makes it work that competitors do not have or cannot easily replicate?
- What does the user experience look like from first touchpoint to core value moment?
- Is there a free tier, trial, or freemium model?
- What integrations or platforms does it connect with?
- What is still being built or on the roadmap, if public?
- What is the most common point of friction or confusion in the product, based on reviews?
- **Insight prompt:** What does the product do that it has not yet figured out how to talk about clearly? This is often where the best positioning lives.
- **Source check:** Separate confirmed features from roadmap items. Do not mix what exists today with what is planned.

---

### SECTION 5 — TRACTION AND PROOF

Only include what is publicly stated. Do not estimate or extrapolate.

- Known metrics: users, customers, revenue, growth rate (cite the source and date for each)
- Notable customers or case studies referenced publicly
- Awards, press features, or recognition (with links)
- Customer testimonials or review site ratings, with specific quotes and sources
- Retention, NPS, or outcome data if publicly available
- What customers say is the single best thing about the product (pull direct quotes)
- What customers say is the biggest frustration or missing piece (pull direct quotes)
- **Source check:** Note confidence level for each metric (verified by company, third-party reported, or estimated by press).

---

### SECTION 6 — AUDIENCE SEGMENTS

Identify each meaningful customer segment. For each:

- Who they are: role, company type, stage, size
- What they are trying to accomplish
- What they are afraid of or frustrated by, in their own words
- The specific language they use to describe their problem (pull from reviews, forums, social)
- How they currently discover and evaluate solutions like this one
- Why they choose this product over alternatives
- Why they might not choose it, or where they churn

If the company targets multiple distinct audiences (e.g., SMB versus enterprise, or two different buyer types), treat each as a separate segment entry.

**Insight prompt:** Is there a segment this company is clearly serving well that they are not explicitly marketing to? Is there a segment they are marketing to that the product does not actually serve as well as they claim? Name both if they exist.

**Source check:** Note which audience insights come from company claims versus validated customer behavior.

---

### SECTION 7 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

Identify the top 5 to 8 competitors. For each:

- Company name and website
- What they actually do, in plain language
- Their primary positioning message (direct quote from homepage if possible)
- Pricing model if public
- Primary strengths
- Known weaknesses and customer complaints (pull from reviews, with quotes)
- Visual identity and brand feel: colors, tone, overall aesthetic
- Who they are built for
- How they talk about themselves versus how customers actually describe them

**Landscape synthesis (required, not optional):**

This is where the research earns its value. Do not just list competitors. Analyze the space.

- What is the dominant positioning in this category? What is everyone saying?
- What words, promises, and frames repeat across every competitor? (These are table stakes, not differentiators.)
- Where is the whitespace? What is nobody saying that could be said and validated?
- What emotional territory is completely unclaimed?
- Who is the market leader and why specifically?
- What does a competitor appear to be doing well but is actually a long-term liability?
- What does winning in this category actually require, beyond what everyone is already offering?
- What is the one insight about this competitive landscape that would genuinely surprise the client?

**Source check:** Note which competitive claims are sourced from competitor sites, third-party reviews, or press. Flag any competitor where information is thin.

---

### SECTION 8 — CURRENT BRAND AND MESSAGING AUDIT

Audit the company's existing brand presence with an honest eye. The goal is not to flatter. It is to see clearly.

- Current tagline or hero headline (direct quote, with URL)
- What problem they are leading with
- Primary CTA
- Tone: formal, casual, technical, inspirational, or mixed
- Words and phrases that repeat most often across site, social, and press
- Words and phrases they should probably be using but are not
- Consistency of messaging across website, social, and press
- Visual identity today: colors, fonts, logo style, overall aesthetic
- What is working about the current brand
- What feels off, underdeveloped, or misaligned with where the company is going
- Any brand assets or design decisions that stand out as genuinely strong

**Insight prompt:** If this company's brand were a person, who would they be? Who are they trying to be? Where is the gap between those two? Name it directly.

**Source check:** Every observation about tone and messaging should be traceable to a specific page, post, or asset.

---

### SECTION 9 — VERBAL IDENTITY INPUTS

This section feeds brand voice and messaging development. The quality of what goes in here directly determines the quality of the verbal identity that comes out.

- 10 to 15 direct quotes from founders across interviews, podcasts, LinkedIn posts, and social media. The goal is to capture how they naturally speak: vocabulary, cadence, what they care about, what they reach for when they are trying to explain something. Cite source and date for each.
- 5 to 10 customer reviews or testimonials that use the customer's own unfiltered language. Do not clean them up.
- Brand language that already feels authentic and ownable: phrases, framings, or metaphors the company uses that feel genuinely theirs
- Language they use that sounds like everyone else in the space (these are phrases to retire)
- 3 to 5 brand or company references the founders or team have mentioned as inspiration, if findable

**Insight prompt:** What is the one phrase or idea this company keeps circling back to across all their communications that they have not yet built a message around explicitly? That is often the brand anchor waiting to be named.

---

### SECTION 10 — VISUAL IDENTITY INPUTS

- Current logo description: shape, style, weight, distinctive features
- Primary brand colors with hex codes if determinable from the site
- Typography in use (identify fonts where possible)
- Overall visual aesthetic: what does it feel like?
- Key visual motifs or design patterns that repeat across materials
- What is worth preserving in the current visual direction
- What feels dated, generic, or misaligned with where the brand is going
- Visual inspiration brands the team has mentioned, if any
- Competitor visual identity for greenspace mapping: what colors and aesthetics are already owned in this category, and what is visually unclaimed

**Insight prompt:** Is there a visual direction that would be completely unoccupied in this competitive space and also genuinely true to who this company is? Describe it.

---

### SECTION 11 — GO-TO-MARKET AND DISTRIBUTION

- How they are currently acquiring customers
- Active channels and approximate quality of presence on each
- Sales motion: self-serve, sales-led, product-led growth, or hybrid
- Economic buyer versus end user (are they the same person, or different?)
- Typical sales cycle or conversion path, if inferable from public information
- Known partnerships, integrations, or distribution deals
- Events, communities, or platforms where they are visible
- Go-to-market signals in job listings or public announcements
- **Insight prompt:** Where is there a clear distribution channel this company is underusing or ignoring entirely that their competitors are also ignoring? Underused channels in a space are often the fastest path to differentiated growth.

---

### SECTION 12 — STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND TIMING

- Why does this company exist now? What changed in the market to make this the right moment?
- Macro trends working in their favor
- Risks and headwinds: competitive, regulatory, behavioral, or market-based
- Major recent events: funding, pivots, leadership changes, product launches
- What they are clearly optimistic about in public communications
- What they are clearly worried about, if readable between the lines
- **Insight prompt:** What is the one external trend that, if it accelerates, makes this company significantly more valuable? What is the one trend that, if it reverses, is an existential threat? Name both.

---

### SECTION 13 — GAPS AND FLAGS FOR HUMAN REVIEW

Close the research with a candid, specific summary. This section is for the human reviewer, not the client. Be direct.

- Which sections above are thin, uncertain, or based on inference rather than verified evidence? List them by section number.
- What specific questions should be answered in a discovery call or supplemental brief? List them as questions the human should ask.
- What information is simply not publicly available and must come from the client?
- What is the single most important thing learned about this company that should shape everything downstream?
- What is the single biggest brand or positioning opportunity visible in the landscape? Commit to an answer. Do not hedge.
- Is there anything about this company, their market, or their competitive position that does not add up or seems inconsistent? Name it.

---

## FINAL INSTRUCTIONS

Specificity beats polish. A rough answer with a source is more useful than a clean answer with no basis.

Pull direct quotes wherever possible. Cite every source.

Do not fill gaps with guesses. Name the gap, explain why it matters, and flag it for the human reviewer.

The gaps section is not an afterthought. It is part of the deliverable.

Format the output so it can be dropped directly into a structured document. Use the section headers exactly as written above.

The goal is not a report. The goal is a foundation a brand strategist can build from without starting over.
