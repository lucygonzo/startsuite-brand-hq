# Empathy Profile: Startup Founder

**Tier:** 1 -- Startups
**Archetype:** The Founder Doing Everything (Archetype 1)
**Data Depth:** Deep

---

## Meet Javi Moreno

**Age:** 31
**Location:** Austin, TX (moved from San Francisco 18 months ago for lower burn rate)
**Company:** Fieldkit -- a B2B SaaS tool for field service teams to manage scheduling and dispatch
**Funding:** Raised $1.8M seed round 4 months ago from a syndicate lead + 3 angels
**Team:** 5 people (Javi as CEO, a CTO, 2 engineers, 1 customer success/ops hire)
**Role:** CEO, but also head of sales, head of marketing, brand manager, website editor, pitch deck designer, social media poster, and the person who picks the fonts

---

## Daily Life

Javi wakes up at 6:30am and checks Slack before his feet hit the floor. There are 3 messages from his CTO about a deployment issue and a customer email asking about a feature that doesn't exist yet. He makes coffee while scanning LinkedIn -- two competitors posted product updates this week and their branding looks clean, professional, cohesive. His looks like it was thrown together in Canva at midnight. Because it was.

By 8am he's on a customer call. By 9:30 he's reviewing a sales deck he built in Google Slides using a template he found on Slidesgo. The fonts don't match the website. The green in the logo is different from the green on the landing page. He knows this is a problem but doesn't know how to fix it without spending a day he doesn't have.

At 11am his investor sends a text: "Saw your LinkedIn post. The brand feels inconsistent. You should tighten that up before the demo day." His stomach drops. He's been meaning to "do something about the brand" for two months. He opened Canva's brand kit, got halfway through, and abandoned it.

Lunch is eaten at his desk while he writes a cold outbound email. He spends 15 minutes agonizing over whether the tone sounds right. He doesn't have a brand voice document. He doesn't have a messaging framework. He has vibes and instinct.

At 3pm he has a call with a potential channel partner who asks to see "brand guidelines." Javi sends them the logo in 3 file formats and a color hex code he saved in a Notion doc. He knows this is embarrassing but doesn't know what a real brand guide looks like.

By 7pm he's home but still working. At 11pm he Googles "startup branding agency affordable" and "brand guide template." He finds 40 Fiverr gigs, 15 agencies that all look the same, and a Canva template that reminds him of the one he already abandoned. He closes the laptop and goes to bed feeling behind.

## How They Find Creative Solutions

**Who they ask first:** Other founders. Javi posts in his accelerator's Slack channel: "Anyone have a good brand person? Not looking for an agency, just someone who gets startups." He gets 4 replies in 2 hours -- 3 freelancer recommendations and 1 "just use Canva Pro."

**What they Google at 11pm:** "startup brand guide template," "how much does branding cost for a startup," "brand agency vs freelancer," "do I need a brand strategy," "Canva vs hiring a designer"

**Communities they check:** YC Startup School forum, Indie Hackers, their seed fund's portfolio Slack, r/startups, Twitter/X threads from founders they follow

**How referrals work:** A founder they trust says "I used [X] and it was great" -- that carries 10x more weight than any marketing. The warm intro is everything. Cold outreach from agencies goes to spam.

---

## How They Decide

**Speed is the primary filter.** If it takes longer than 2 weeks, Javi will find another way. He cannot afford to wait 8 weeks for a brand agency to deliver.

**Portfolio is the trust signal.** He wants to see work done for companies that look like his -- early-stage, tech, clean, modern. If the portfolio is full of restaurants and law firms, he's gone in 10 seconds.

**Price is a gate, not a differentiator.** He'll pay $3K--$5K for brand work if he believes it will save him time and make him look credible to investors. He will not pay $15K. The gap between "expensive" and "worth it" is entirely determined by speed and proof.

**The referral from another founder is the closer.** If his accelerator batchmate says "StartSuite built our brand in 10 days and our investors noticed," the deal is 80% done before the first call.

## Frustrations

1. **"I have 3 different greens on my website and I don't know which one is right."** -- Visual inconsistency is the symptom they notice first. Lucy saw this exact problem with 2047 Sports.

2. **"My investor told me the brand looks amateur but didn't tell me what to do about it."** -- Investor feedback on brand is common but never actionable. Founders feel judged without a path forward.

3. **"I spent 4 hours in Canva and it still doesn't look professional."** -- The DIY trap. They get 80% there and hit a wall on the last 20% of polish and consistency.

4. **"I don't know what a brand guide is supposed to include."** -- They've heard the term but have never seen a real one. When they see Brand HQ for the first time, this is a "spark in the eye" moment.

5. **"Every agency I talked to wants $15K minimum and 8 weeks."** -- Traditional agency timelines and pricing are built for mid-market, not startups. Founders feel priced out of professional brand work.

6. **"I'm using ChatGPT for copy but everything sounds generic."** -- AI tools produce output without brand context. The result is competent but personality-less. Tracy described prompting Claude "100 times" to get something usable.

7. **"I don't have time to explain my brand to every freelancer."** -- Context transfer is exhausting. Every new designer or writer requires a download that takes hours and still results in off-brand work.

## Trust Signals -- What Builds Credibility

| Signal | Weight | Why |
|--------|--------|-----|
| 46 Capital backing | HIGH | VC validation. If an investor trusts StartSuite, founders trust StartSuite. |
| Named startup portfolio | HIGH | "They did the brand for [company I've heard of]" is the strongest signal. |
| Founder testimonials (specific) | HIGH | Not "great experience" -- but "they built our brand in 10 days and our Series A deck looked like a $50M company." |
| Speed-of-delivery proof | HIGH | "Built in 2 days" or "rebranded in 2 weeks" -- specific timeframes that prove speed. |
| The Brand HQ demo | HIGHEST | Seeing a live brand guide as a website (not a PDF) is the single biggest conversion moment. |
| LinkedIn thought leadership | MEDIUM | Consistent posting that demonstrates taste and strategic depth. Not selling -- teaching. |
| Price transparency | MEDIUM | Showing starting-at pricing on the website filters out unqualified leads and builds trust with qualified ones. |

## Journey Stages with Leaks

### Stage 1: Awareness
**How they first hear about StartSuite:** A founder in their accelerator Slack mentions it. Or they see a LinkedIn post from Reagan or Lucy that resonates. Or Tracy recommends it to a portfolio company.
**The leak:** If StartSuite isn't present in the founder communities where recommendations happen, they never hear about it. Cold outreach to founders is largely ignored. The awareness channel is warm referral, not marketing.
**Fix:** Seed every accelerator cohort with one client. That client becomes the referral source for the rest of the cohort.

### Stage 2: Research
**What they look at:** Website (30 seconds to decide), portfolio/case studies, pricing page, LinkedIn profiles of the founders, any social proof (logos, testimonials, investor backing).
**What's missing:** Case studies with specific startup metrics. Before/after visuals. Clear pricing. A "how it works" that takes 60 seconds to understand.
**The leak:** The website doesn't answer "what does this cost?" and "what will I get?" fast enough. Founders who can't figure it out in 2 minutes leave.
**Fix:** Homepage needs: (1) one sentence that describes the pain, (2) 3 portfolio examples, (3) starting-at pricing, (4) a "book a call" CTA. Nothing else.

### Stage 3: Decision
**What tips them over:** Seeing Brand HQ live for a company similar to theirs. The "spark in the eye" moment. A referral from someone they trust. Speed guarantee ("we deliver in 10 business days").
**What holds them back:** Budget uncertainty ("can I afford this for 12 months?"), DIY confidence ("maybe I can just do this myself"), timing ("we're in the middle of a sprint, maybe next month"), and the classic "I need to talk to my co-founder."
**The leak:** No urgency. Brand work doesn't have a deadline like a product launch. Founders push it to "someday."
**Fix:** Tie brand work to a forcing function: "Your demo day is in 6 weeks. Your pitch deck is your brand. Let's make it right before you're on stage."

### Stage 4: Onboarding (First 30 Days)
**Experience:** Brand Discovery kicks off. Javi has a strategy call, provides context about the business, and within 1--2 weeks sees a Brand HQ draft. The first time he sees his competitive landscape research, his brand voice defined, and a visual system that actually works -- this is the "holy shit" moment.
**The leak:** If the first 2 weeks feel slow or unclear, founder confidence drops fast. Startups expect startup speed from their vendors.
**Fix:** Deliver a visible artifact within 5 business days. Even a draft competitive landscape or brand voice document. Show momentum immediately.

### Stage 5: Retention
**What keeps them paying:** Monthly strategy calls that surface insights they wouldn't find alone. Competitive intelligence updates. The compounding context effect -- every month, Brand HQ knows more about their brand. Content outputs that actually sound like them.
**The leak:** If Foundations feels like "just a brand guide we already have," there's no reason to keep paying $3K/mo. The value must be active, not static.
**Fix:** Weekly touchpoints. Proactive recommendations ("your competitor just launched a new positioning -- here's what it means for you"). Make Brand HQ feel alive, not archived.

### Stage 6: Advocacy
**What makes them refer:** A visible transformation they're proud to share. "Look at what StartSuite built for us" posted on LinkedIn with before/after. Being asked "who did your brand?" and having a name to give.
**The leak:** If the transformation isn't visible or shareable, founders have nothing to point to when asked. The work needs to be showcase-worthy.
**Fix:** Build a "launch moment" into every engagement. A LinkedIn post template. A before/after asset they can share. Make referral frictionless.
