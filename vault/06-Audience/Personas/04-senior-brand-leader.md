# Persona Profile: The Senior Brand Leader

**Persona:** 04 -- Senior Brand Leader
**Primary Tier:** Tier 4 (Enterprise)
**Secondary Tier:** Tier 3 (Established Companies)
**Overlays:** Time-Starved (moderate), AI-Overwhelmed (low -- more AI-curious)
**Based on:** Jed / Costa Sunglasses archetype

---

## Meet Catherine Park

**Age:** 46
**Title:** VP of Brand Strategy, Meridian Outdoor (premium outdoor apparel and gear, subsidiary of a European luxury group, $350M revenue)
**Company:** 800 employees. Brand team of 12. Works with 3 external agencies (creative, media, digital). Publicly traded parent company.
**Reports to:** CMO (who reports to the CEO).
**Location:** Portland, OR. Lives in the West Hills with her husband (architect) and their 14-year-old son.
**Education:** Northwestern (journalism undergrad), NYU Stern (MBA). 22 years in brand marketing. Previous roles: Brand Manager at Patagonia (4 years), Senior Director at Nike (6 years), Head of Brand at a DTC outdoor startup that got acquired (3 years).
**Personal:** Trail runner. Reads The New Yorker and Monocle. Collects mid-century ceramics. Has a cabin at Mt. Hood where she goes to "think without Slack."

## Day in the Life

**6:00 AM** -- Up before the alarm. Coffee from the Chemex (she's particular about this). Reads the morning briefs: WWD, Business of Fashion, and a Google Alert for "Meridian Outdoor" plus competitors. A competitor just announced a brand collaboration with a music festival. She makes a mental note.

**7:00 AM** -- Drive to the office (she goes in 4 days/week). Listens to nothing. This is her thinking time. She's working through a positioning problem: the European parent company wants to push Meridian more "premium" to align with the group's other brands, but the US customer base associates Meridian with accessibility and adventure. She needs to thread the needle without alienating either audience.

**8:00 AM** -- At her desk. Reviews the creative brief her team prepared for the fall campaign. It's good but not great. The strategic insight is too safe. She rewrites the core tension section herself and sends it back with a note: "Dig deeper. What's the emotional truth here? Not what we want to say -- what the customer actually feels."

**8:45 AM** -- Video call with the European HQ. The group brand director wants alignment on visual identity across all subsidiaries. She pushes back: "Meridian's visual language works because it's rooted in the Pacific Northwest outdoor culture. If we adopt the European minimalism that works for the luxury handbag division, we'll look inauthentic to our core customer." The call goes 20 minutes over. She wins the argument. For now.

**9:30 AM** -- One-on-one with her senior brand manager. They discuss the competitive landscape: two competitors are leaning hard into AI-generated content. One has visibly dropped in quality. The other is using AI in interesting ways -- dynamic personalization on their website, AI-curated styling recommendations. Catherine wants to understand the technology without getting sucked into hype. She asks: "What can we learn from what they're doing without becoming them?"

**10:00 AM** -- Agency review meeting. The creative agency presents campaign concepts. Three directions. Direction 1 is safe. Direction 2 is interesting but execution-dependent. Direction 3 is risky and brilliant. Catherine knows the CMO will choose Direction 1 unless she builds the case for Direction 3. She spends the next hour preparing that case.

**11:30 AM** -- Walks to the test kitchen (Meridian has an on-site demo space) to check the setup for an influencer event next week. The experiential team has it under control. She makes one adjustment: "Move the product wall so the natural light hits it. The photography will be 10x better." Twenty years of brand instinct.

**12:30 PM** -- Lunch with a former colleague from Nike who's now consulting. They talk about the state of the industry. He mentions a company that's building "brand intelligence systems" -- AI-powered competitive analysis, brand voice management, strategic positioning engines. Catherine is intrigued but cautious. She's seen a dozen martech tools promise transformation and deliver dashboards nobody looks at. "If it's another dashboard, I don't need it. If it actually thinks strategically, I'm interested."

**1:30 PM** -- Reviews Q1 brand health metrics. NPS is up. Brand awareness in the 25-34 demo is up 8 points. But social sentiment around the recent pricing increase is negative. She writes a brief for the comms team: how to reframe the price increase as a quality investment without sounding defensive.

**2:30 PM** -- Cross-functional meeting with product design, merchandising, and retail. The product team wants to launch a lower-priced line to compete with mass market. Catherine argues this will dilute the brand position. She presents 3 historical case studies of premium brands that launched discount lines and lost brand equity. The product VP pushes back. The meeting ends without resolution. She'll need data to settle this.

**3:30 PM** -- Reviews competitive intelligence from her analyst. It's a 15-page deck. Good data but it took 3 weeks to compile. She wishes this were updated weekly, not quarterly. She wishes the strategic implications were drawn out, not just the data tables. She wishes she didn't need an analyst to spend 3 weeks on something that should be automated.

**4:30 PM** -- Mentors a junior brand manager for 30 minutes. Gives feedback on a brand extension proposal. Teaches her how to structure a strategic argument: "Start with the tension. Then the insight. Then the recommendation. Never start with the recommendation."

**5:00 PM** -- Wraps up email. One from the CMO: "The board wants a 'brand value' number for the annual report. Can you put something together?" Catherine sighs. Brand valuation is a fraught exercise. She'll do it, but she knows whatever number she presents will be scrutinized by people who don't understand brand measurement.

**6:30 PM** -- Home. Dinner with her family. Her son talks about a school project on sustainability. She makes a mental connection to Meridian's sustainability positioning and writes a note on her phone.

**9:00 PM** -- In her home office. Scrolls through the brand intelligence company her former colleague mentioned at lunch. The website is clean. The case studies are from companies she respects. But the product feels like it's designed for mid-market companies, not enterprises with 12-person brand teams and $5M agency budgets. She thinks: "I don't need someone to build my brand. I need someone who can match my thinking and multiply my team's effectiveness." She bookmarks it and moves on.

## Decision Triggers

1. **Strategic depth in the first conversation.** Catherine decides in the first 15 minutes whether a potential partner can match her sophistication. If the StartSuite team leads with strategic insight about her brand (not generic positioning advice), she leans in.
2. **Competitive intelligence quality.** If StartSuite delivers competitive analysis that surfaces insights her 3-week analyst process misses, that's the proof point. Speed AND depth.
3. **The team multiplier framing.** Catherine isn't looking for a vendor. She's looking for a force multiplier. The pitch must be: "We make your team of 12 perform like a team of 20" -- not "we replace your agencies."
4. **Peer validation.** If another VP-level brand leader at a company she respects uses StartSuite, that's the strongest signal. She trusts the network above all else.
5. **The "finally, someone who thinks" moment.** Most vendors pitch execution. If StartSuite pitches strategic intelligence, Catherine recognizes it immediately because she's been asking for it for years.
