# Persona Profile: The Portfolio Partner

**Persona:** 05 -- Portfolio Partner
**Primary Tier:** Tier 5 (Investors & Accelerators)
**Secondary Tier:** Tier 1 (Startups -- as a referral channel)
**Overlays:** Time-Starved (moderate), Credibility-Seeking (for portfolio companies, not self)
**Based on:** Tracy / 46 Capital archetype

---

## Meet Damon Hartwell

**Age:** 47
**Title:** Managing Partner, Forge Ventures (early-stage B2B fund, $18M Fund II) & Program Director, Forge Accelerator (2 cohorts/year, 10 companies each)
**Fund:** Seed and pre-seed B2B SaaS. Based in Oklahoma City. Invests in non-coastal startups -- flyover country founders building real businesses.
**Portfolio:** 24 active companies across 2 funds. Average check size $250K--$500K. Plus 20 accelerator alumni companies per year.
**Team:** Damon + 1 associate + 1 operations/admin person. No platform team. No in-house marketing support.
**Location:** Oklahoma City, OK. Grew up in Stillwater. OSU grad. Never left the state and doesn't plan to. His pitch to LPs: "The best founders are out here. They just don't have the infrastructure San Francisco founders take for granted."
**Personal:** Married to an ER nurse. Three kids (12, 15, 17). Coaches high school basketball in the winter. Active in the OKC startup community. Knows everyone.

## Day in the Life

**6:00 AM** -- Up. Coffee from a drip machine (no pour-over pretensions). Opens his phone. LinkedIn first -- it's where he lives professionally. Scrolls through portfolio company updates. One founder posted about a new customer win. Damon likes it and adds a comment. Another founder hasn't posted in 6 weeks. He texts her: "Hey, you need to be visible. Post something."

**6:30 AM** -- Scans his email. LP update is due in 2 weeks. He needs portfolio company data: revenue, hiring, fundraising status, key milestones. He sends a template to all 24 companies. He knows half of them will respond late and a quarter won't respond at all.

**7:00 AM** -- Drives to the co-working space that serves as Forge's office. Listens to 20VC. Today's episode features a fund that built a full "platform team" -- internal designers, recruiters, and marketing people who serve portfolio companies. Damon thinks: "Must be nice. I have three people including me."

**7:45 AM** -- First meeting: a portfolio company founder practicing her Series A pitch. The company builds compliance software for rural hospitals. Strong product. Growing revenue. The pitch deck is 28 slides. Slide 1 has a blurry logo. The colors change between slides 4 and 12. Slide 18 is a wall of text in 10-point font. Damon says: "The story is great. The deck is killing it. Can you get a designer to clean this up?" She says: "I've been trying. Our freelancer ghosted us and we can't afford an agency." Damon adds it to his mental list of "things I wish I could fix at scale."

**9:00 AM** -- Accelerator cohort standup (current batch, 10 companies). Each gives a 2-minute update. Five of them mention needing "marketing help." One shows a website that looks like a high school project. Another has a logo that his 12-year-old could have made on an iPad. Damon makes encouraging noises but internally calculates: "These companies are pitching at demo day in 8 weeks and half of them look like they started yesterday."

**10:00 AM** -- Call with a potential LP for Fund III. The LP asks about portfolio support. "What do you do beyond the check?" Damon talks about introductions, office hours, the accelerator curriculum. The LP probes: "What about marketing and brand? I see a lot of early funds investing in portfolio support for go-to-market." Damon says: "That's something we're building out." He's not building it out. He has no idea how to build it out.

**11:00 AM** -- Tries to find Forge's own logo file for a conference presentation. Opens the shared Google Drive. There's a folder called "Forge Logos" with 23 files. He opens 4 of them before finding one that isn't pixelated. He texts his admin: "Can you figure out which logo is the real one? The one with the gradient or the flat one?" She responds: "I think both? We used the gradient on the website but the flat one on the business cards." He picks the flat one because he's out of patience.

**12:00 PM** -- Lunch with another fund manager from Dallas. They compare notes. The Dallas fund just partnered with a startup that provides "brand infrastructure" to portfolio companies. Each company gets a brand system built in their first month. Damon's interest spikes. "What does that cost?" The friend says: "Less than what we were spending on ad-hoc design help across the portfolio." Damon takes a photo of the business card.

**1:30 PM** -- Office hours with 3 portfolio founders (30 min each). Founder 1 needs help with pricing strategy. Damon is great at this -- he's done it 50 times. Founder 2 needs help with hiring. Damon makes 2 introductions. Founder 3 says: "We need to completely redo our brand before our next raise. Every investor we talk to says we don't look serious." Damon nods and says: "Let me think about how we can help with this." He has no solution. He's been hearing this from portfolio founders for 3 years.

**3:00 PM** -- Reviews demo day schedule. 8 weeks out. 10 companies presenting. He mentally rates their readiness: 3 are ready. 4 need work. 3 are in trouble -- and in all 3 cases, the brand/pitch presentation is the weakest element, not the product.

**4:00 PM** -- Quarterly portfolio review. Pulls up the portfolio tracker. Scans websites for the 24 active companies. He visits each one. Some look great. Some look like they haven't been updated since 2024. Two have broken links. One has a competitor's stock photo on the homepage. He thinks about what LPs see when they Google his portfolio companies. It's not good.

**5:00 PM** -- Heads home. Calls a portfolio founder on the drive. She just lost a major partnership because the partner "didn't feel confident in the brand." She's frustrated. Damon is frustrated. He's been hearing variations of this for years and doesn't have a scalable answer.

**6:30 PM** -- Family dinner. His 15-year-old shows him a TikTok of a startup with amazing branding. "Dad, why don't your companies look like this?" He explains that design costs money. His son says: "Can't AI do it?" Damon thinks: "Not in a way that actually works. Not yet."

**9:00 PM** -- Opens his laptop. Looks up the brand infrastructure company his Dallas fund manager friend mentioned at lunch. Watches a demo. Sees the concept: a brand operating system that deploys in days, built on AI intelligence, with a decision audit log and competitive analysis built in. He sees portfolio pricing. He does the math: "If I spent $1,500/month per company for the 10 accelerator companies, that's $15K/month for 3 months during the program. $45K total. I spent more than that on ad-hoc design help last year -- and got nothing systematic from it."

Then he sees the framing: "Like QuickBooks for your brand." And something clicks. He thinks about how every startup gets QuickBooks, Gusto, and Stripe in Week 1. Brand should be next. Not "nice to have at Series A." A requirement from Day 1.

He sends a text to his associate: "Set up a call with these guys. I want to explore a portfolio deal."

**10:00 PM** -- Bed. Falls asleep thinking: "If I can tell LPs that every company in our portfolio gets institutional-grade brand infrastructure as part of the Forge platform, that's a differentiator. That's a reason to invest in Fund III over the other 50 seed funds out there."

## Decision Triggers

1. **The QuickBooks moment.** Someone frames brand infrastructure as essential startup tooling -- like accounting, like payroll, like legal. Damon immediately sees the portfolio-wide application.
2. **The demo day frustration.** Watching strong companies fail at demo day because their presentation looks amateur. The product is ready. The brand isn't. This frustration has been building for 3 years.
3. **The LP question.** An LP asks "what do you do beyond the check?" and Damon realizes he needs a better answer than "we make introductions."
4. **The peer recommendation.** Another fund manager says "we're using this for our portfolio." Damon trusts peer fund managers above all other sources.
5. **The portfolio pricing math.** When he sees that deploying StartSuite across 10 companies costs less than what he spent on ad-hoc design help last year, the decision is economic, not emotional.

## Trust Signals That Convert

| Signal | Why It Works |
|--------|-------------|
| Portfolio-level pricing | "The math works at scale. I can budget this across the fund." |
| Speed ("Brand HQ in days, not months") | His founders don't have time. Days is credible. Months is disqualifying. |
| The QuickBooks comparison | Reframes brand from optional creative work to essential infrastructure. |
| Before/after portfolio proof | "Show me a fund that deployed this and what changed." Visual proof + fundraising outcomes. |
| 46Capital association / Tracy warm intro | If Tracy is using it, it's vetted. VC-to-VC trust is the strongest signal. |
| "Fund gets quarterly brand health reports" | Gives Damon something to show LPs. Makes his fund look sophisticated. |
| Pilot option (3 companies, 30 days) | Low risk. High information value. Lets him evaluate before committing the portfolio. |
