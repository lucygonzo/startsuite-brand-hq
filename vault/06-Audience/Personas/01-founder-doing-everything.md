# Persona Profile: The Founder Doing Everything

**Persona:** 01 -- Founder Doing Everything
**Primary Tier:** Tier 1 (Startups)
**Secondary Tier:** Tier 5 (via Accelerator referral)
**Overlays:** Time-Starved (strong), Credibility-Seeking (strong), AI-Overwhelmed (moderate)

---

## Meet Priya Mehta

**Age:** 31
**Title:** Co-founder & CEO, Canopy Health (digital health platform for chronic care management)
**Company stage:** Seed. Just closed a $1.8M round led by a Tulsa-based health-tech fund.
**Team:** 4 people. Priya (CEO/product), her co-founder Raj (CTO/engineering), a full-stack developer, and a part-time customer success hire.
**Location:** Austin, TX. Relocated from San Francisco 18 months ago for lower burn rate.
**Education:** Stanford undergrad (biology), worked at McKinsey for 3 years, then joined a health-tech startup that got acquired.
**Personal:** Single. Lives in a 1BR apartment in East Austin. Runs 3 miles every morning at 5:45 AM because it's the only time she controls.

---

## Day in the Life

**5:45 AM** -- Alarm. Running shoes already by the bed. She runs the same route every morning because one fewer decision matters. Listens to Lenny's Podcast or First Round Review while running. Today's episode is about positioning. She makes a mental note to "fix our positioning" and immediately forgets.

**7:00 AM** -- Shower, coffee, opens Slack. 14 unread messages. Raj pushed a product update overnight. The developer has a question about the API. The customer success person forwarded a complaint from a user who can't figure out the onboarding flow. Priya responds to all of them in 12 minutes.

**7:30 AM** -- Opens her pitch deck. She has an investor update due Friday and a warm intro meeting with a Series A fund at 2 PM. The deck is 22 slides. She made it in Google Slides. The fonts are inconsistent. The colors don't match the website. The logo on slide 1 is a different shade of green than the logo on the website. She knows this looks bad but doesn't know how to fix it without spending a day in Canva.

**8:15 AM** -- Checks LinkedIn. A founder she knows from the accelerator just posted about their rebrand. The before/after is dramatic. They look like they raised $10M. She knows they raised $1.5M. She feels a pang of inadequacy and closes the app.

**9:00 AM** -- Product standup with the team (15 minutes on Zoom). Raj mentions that a potential enterprise customer asked for "a brand guidelines document" before they'd proceed with a pilot. Priya says "I'll handle it." She has no brand guidelines document. She has a logo that a freelancer made on Fiverr for $200 and a color palette she picked from Coolors.co.

**9:30 AM** -- Opens ChatGPT. Types: "Help me write brand guidelines for a digital health startup." Gets a generic 2-page output that sounds like every other startup. She tries to make it better with follow-up prompts. After 40 minutes, she has something that's technically "brand guidelines" but feels hollow. There's no strategic foundation behind it. She saves it as a Google Doc and moves on.

**10:30 AM** -- Customer calls. Two back-to-back user interviews for a new feature. This is what she's actually good at -- understanding the problem, talking to users, shaping the product. For 90 minutes, she forgets about brand entirely.

**12:00 PM** -- Lunch at her desk. Scrolls Twitter/X. Sees a thread about "startup brand mistakes that kill fundraising." Screenshots it. Adds it to a Note on her phone titled "Brand To-Do" that has 47 other screenshots.

**12:30 PM** -- Tries to update the company website. It's on Webflow. She sort of knows how to use it. She changes the hero headline, but the new text doesn't fit the layout and now the mobile version looks broken. She spends 20 minutes trying to fix it, gives up, and reverts.

**1:00 PM** -- Prep for the 2 PM investor meeting. Re-reads the fund's portfolio page. Practices her pitch out loud. Realizes her "why now" slide is weak. Tries to write a better one. Stares at the blank slide for 8 minutes. Types something generic. Moves on.

**2:00 PM** -- Investor meeting (Zoom). The conversation goes well -- the fund partner is engaged, asks good questions about the market and product. Then: "Walk me through your go-to-market." Priya talks about her target customer, her outbound strategy, her referral partnerships. The partner says: "And how do you think about brand and positioning as you scale?" Priya freezes for half a second, then gives a vague answer about "content marketing and thought leadership." She can feel the partner mentally downgrading her.

**3:30 PM** -- Post-meeting debrief with herself. She knows the brand question was the weakest part. She Googles: "startup brand agency fast." Sees agencies charging $15K--$50K for a 6-week brand project. Closes the tab. Googles: "pitch deck design service." Sees dozens of options. Doesn't know which one to trust. Closes the tab.

**4:00 PM** -- Back to product work. Responds to Slack. Reviews a PR from the developer. Joins a 30-minute call with a potential channel partner who wants to understand "what Canopy Health stands for." Priya realizes she gives a slightly different answer every time someone asks this question.

**6:00 PM** -- Tries to write a LinkedIn post to build "founder presence." Stares at the blank post for 5 minutes. Writes something. Deletes it. Writes something else. It sounds too corporate. Gives up. Closes LinkedIn.

**7:30 PM** -- Dinner (takeout). Opens the pitch deck again. Spends 2 hours trying to fix the design. Moves text boxes around. Downloads a free pitch deck template. Tries to adapt it. The template's brand colors clash with her logo. She manually changes every slide. At 9:30 PM, the deck looks marginally better but still doesn't feel cohesive.

**10:00 PM** -- In bed scrolling Indie Hackers and YC forums. Someone posted "How I went from zero brand to looking like a Series A company in 2 weeks." She reads it. They used some kind of brand operating system. She bookmarks it.

**10:30 PM** -- Sets alarm for 5:45 AM. Falls asleep thinking: "I need to fix the brand thing. Maybe next week."

## Decision Triggers

1. **Embarrassment at a demo day or investor meeting.** The moment someone looks at her deck and she can see the silent judgment. This is the #1 trigger.
2. **Referral from another founder.** "My friend used this and went from looking amateur to looking Series B." Word-of-mouth is the most trusted signal.
3. **Seeing a before/after.** Visual proof that a company like hers can transform quickly. The contrast has to be dramatic.
4. **The accelerator recommendation.** If her fund or accelerator partner recommends StartSuite, she'll try it without hesitation. Trust transfer from the fund.
5. **The "I can't do this myself" wall.** She's tried Canva, ChatGPT, freelancers. Nothing produces a cohesive system. She hits the wall and is ready to pay for a real solution.

| Founder testimonials | She trusts other founders above all other sources. |
| 46Capital / accelerator association | Institutional credibility makes it feel safe, not risky. |
| "You own everything" framing | She's been burned by freelancers who kept source files. |

## Journey Stages and Leaks

| Stage | What Happens | StartSuite Role | Leak |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|------|
| Unaware | Priya thinks brand = logo + colors. Doesn't realize she has a brand infrastructure problem. | Content that surfaces the symptom: "Why is your pitch deck so hard to make?" | If messaging leads with "brand strategy," Priya scrolls past. She doesn't think she needs strategy -- she thinks she needs a designer. |
| Problem-Aware | She realizes inconsistency is costing her (investor meeting, enterprise customer request). | Symptom-first content: "You're not bad at design. You're missing a system." | If the solution sounds like "hire an agency," she disqualifies on price and time immediately. |
| Solution-Aware | She starts looking for options: agencies, AI tools, freelancers, templates. | SEO and founder community presence. "Startup brand operating system" search intent. | If StartSuite doesn't show up in founder channels (YC, Indie Hackers, Twitter), she never finds it. |
| Evaluation | She visits the site, checks pricing, reads case studies. | Show founder-specific proof: "From seed to Series A-ready in 48 hours." | If the site looks like a traditional agency (portfolio of pretty logos), she bounces. She needs to see a system, not a gallery. |
| Trial | She books a Brand Discovery call. | 90-minute call. Low commitment. High value. The call itself should be the sales moment. | If the call feels like a sales pitch instead of genuine strategic work, she ghosts. |
| Purchase | She signs up for Foundations. | Deliver Brand HQ within 48 hours of the call. Speed is the proof. | If delivery takes 2+ weeks, the urgency fades and she deprioritizes. |
| Retention | Brand HQ becomes her source of truth for everything brand-related. | Weekly updates, competitive intel, content suggestions. Keep the system alive. | If Brand HQ goes stale (no updates, no new intelligence), she cancels after 3 months. |
| Advocacy | She tells other founders. | Give her shareable proof: a link to her Brand HQ, a before/after she can screenshot. | If there's nothing easy to share, she stays a satisfied but silent customer. |
