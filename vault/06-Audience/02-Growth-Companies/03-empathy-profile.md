# Empathy Profile: Growth Company Buyer

**Tier:** 2 -- Growth Companies
**Archetype:** The First Marketing Hire (Archetype 2) / The Marketing Director Managing Chaos (Archetype 3)
**Data Depth:** Deep

---

## Meet Priya Khatri

**Age:** 34
**Location:** Nashville, TN
**Company:** Arcline -- a vertical SaaS platform for commercial property management
**Funding:** Series A ($12M raised 8 months ago). Previously raised $2.5M seed.
**Revenue:** $6M ARR, growing 40% year-over-year
**Team:** 28 people (engineering-heavy: 14 engineers, 4 sales, 3 CS, 2 ops, 1 marketing coordinator, 1 designer, CEO, COO, CFO)
**Priya's role:** VP of Marketing. Hired 5 months ago. First dedicated marketing leader. Reports to the CEO.

---

## Daily Life

Priya's alarm goes off at 6:15am. Before she's out of bed, she's scanning her email for anything from the CEO -- he sends late-night messages when ideas hit him, usually with a link to a competitor's website and the subject line "why don't we look like this?"

By 7:30am she's at her desk reviewing the content calendar. The marketing coordinator, Becca, built it in a Google Sheet. It has 14 columns and no strategy behind it. Priya inherited it and hasn't had time to rebuild it because she's been in back-to-back meetings since her first week.

At 9am she has a standing sync with the CEO. Today he wants to know why the website still says "the future of property management" when they repositioned to "operational intelligence" two months ago. Priya knows it's because the contractor they used to build the site in Webflow is on a different project and hasn't returned her Slack message in 4 days. The CEO also asks about the pitch deck for an upcoming conference -- the fonts don't match the website, the color on slide 7 is different from slide 3, and there's a tagline on the final slide that nobody recognizes.

At 10:30am she meets with the designer, Marcus. He's talented but was hired before Priya arrived and has no brand guidelines to work from. Every request is a negotiation. "What color should this be?" "What's our tone for this audience?" "Is this the right logo version?" Marcus has 3 different logo files on his laptop and isn't sure which one is current. Neither is Priya.

At noon she eats lunch at her desk while trying to write a LinkedIn post for the CEO's account. She has no messaging framework, no brand voice document, and no competitive positioning to reference. She writes 4 drafts, hates all of them, and schedules a generic one that sounds like every other SaaS company.

At 2pm she's on a call with an agency the CEO's co-founder recommended. They want $12K/mo on a 6-month contract. Their portfolio is all DTC brands and restaurants. They ask Priya to "send over the brand guidelines" and she has to admit there aren't any. The silence on the call is loud.

At 4pm Becca asks Priya to review 8 social posts for the week. Three of them use different taglines. One uses a green that doesn't exist anywhere else in the brand. Priya fixes them manually, knowing she'll be doing this again next Monday.

By 7pm she's home but opens her laptop at 9:30pm. She Googles "brand strategy for growing companies" and "how to build a brand guide." She finds agency websites that all say the same thing. She finds Canva templates that feel too basic. She finds a LinkedIn post from someone at StartSuite about a company that had "3 different greens on their website" and stops scrolling. That's her.

## How They Find Creative Solutions

**Who they ask first:** Their professional network. Priya posts in the Exit Five Slack community: "We're 28 people, Series A, no brand guidelines. Do I need an agency, a freelancer, or should I just build this myself?" She gets 12 replies in 3 hours -- half say "hire an agency," half say "build it yourself," one says "look into brand operating systems."

**What they Google:** "brand strategy agency for SaaS," "how to build brand guidelines from scratch," "brand agency vs in-house brand manager," "how much should brand strategy cost," "brand audit checklist"

**Who they benchmark against:** Companies one stage ahead of them. If Arcline is Series A, Priya is studying Series B/C companies' brands. She screenshots competitor websites, saves LinkedIn posts from companies she admires, and builds a "brand inspiration" folder that has 47 screenshots and no strategy.

**How referrals work:** A peer VP of Marketing at another portfolio company says "we used [X] and it changed how we operate." That carries enormous weight. The shared experience of being the "first marketing hire at a growing company" creates immediate trust.

---

## How They Decide

**The internal sell matters more than the external pitch.** Priya may be convinced after one Brand HQ demo. But she needs to sell the CEO, who will ask "why can't Marcus just do this?" and the CFO, who will ask "what's the ROI on $3K/mo?" Priya needs ammunition.

**Proof of concept is the fastest path.** If StartSuite can build a mini Brand HQ or competitive analysis for Arcline in 2 days (like Lucy did for 2047 Sports), Priya can walk into her CEO's office and show him something tangible instead of pitching a concept.

**Flexibility beats packages.** Priya has been burned by agencies with rigid scopes. She wants to start with one thing, prove value, and expand. A modular model (Foundations + add-ons) matches her buying psychology.

**Speed is expected, not exceptional.** At startup stage, speed is a wow factor. At growth stage, speed is table stakes. Priya expects 1--2 week turnarounds because that's the pace her company operates at. Anything slower feels like the agency world she's trying to avoid.

## Frustrations

1. **"Nobody defined the brand before me, and now I'm supposed to execute from nothing."** -- Priya inherited a logo, a color palette (maybe), and vibes. No voice guide, no messaging framework, no competitive positioning. Every decision requires a conversation that should have been settled before she was hired.

2. **"I have a designer but no system for him to work from."** -- Marcus is good at design. He's not a brand strategist. He needs a framework to interpret, not a blank canvas to fill. Without Brand HQ, every design request becomes a strategy discussion disguised as a "quick ask."

3. **"I can't defend my decisions to the CEO because I don't have data."** -- When the CEO asks "why did we use this tagline?" Priya has no audit trail, no competitive analysis, no documented rationale. She makes gut calls and hopes they're right.

4. **"Our sub-brands / product lines don't feel connected."** -- Like PhyCap with Robin and Dr. Slowsar: the sub-brands were created ad hoc by different people at different times. There's no parent architecture, no shared visual system, no voice governance.

5. **"Every agency I talk to wants $12K/mo and a 6-month lock-in."** -- The agency model is built for mid-market companies with $500K+ marketing budgets. At $6M ARR, Priya's total marketing budget is $300K--$500K. A $12K/mo retainer eats 30--50% of it for one vendor.

6. **"I'm spending 40% of my time on brand infrastructure that should already exist."** -- Priya was hired to drive growth. Instead, she's building brand guidelines, hunting for the right logo file, standardizing taglines, and explaining to the designer what "our voice" sounds like. She's doing the work StartSuite should be doing.

7. **"AI tools don't know our brand."** -- She's tried ChatGPT for copy. It produces competent but generic output. Every prompt requires a 500-word context dump about the brand, and even then the output sounds like a template. The missing layer is the brand intelligence that makes AI output sound like Arcline, not like "a SaaS company."

## Journey Stages with Leaks

### Stage 1: Awareness
**How they first hear about StartSuite:** A LinkedIn post that names their exact pain ("Your brand was built for 5 people. You're at 30."). A peer recommendation in a professional community. A portfolio company referral from a shared investor.
**The leak:** If StartSuite's content reads as "for startups," Priya self-selects out. She doesn't think of herself as a startup person. She's a marketing leader at a real company. The messaging must reflect scale, not scrappiness.
**Fix:** Segment content. "For companies that outgrew their brand" is different messaging than "for founders on day one."

### Stage 2: Research
**What they look at:** Case studies (first), pricing (second), team bios (third), the Brand HQ demo (if available). They'll also check LinkedIn profiles, look for testimonials from similar companies, and read any blog/podcast content to evaluate strategic depth.
**What's missing:** Growth-stage case studies. Clear pricing comparison (StartSuite vs. agency vs. hire). A "how it works for companies your size" page that addresses the marketing director persona specifically.
**The leak:** The website speaks to founders. Priya is a marketing director. She needs to see her job title, her problems, her use cases reflected. If the site feels like it's for pre-seed companies, she leaves.
**Fix:** Dedicated "Growth Companies" page or section that addresses the "outgrew your brand" problem. Feature Steadwell, 2047, PhyCap as proof points.

### Stage 3: Decision
**What tips them over:** A proof-of-concept deliverable. Lucy built Brand HQ for 2047 in 2 days. If StartSuite can show Priya a draft competitive landscape or brand audit for Arcline, the deal accelerates from "interesting" to "we need this."
**What holds them back:** Internal alignment (CEO, CFO approval). Scope uncertainty ("what exactly do I get?"). Agency fatigue ("is this just going to be another retainer that disappoints?"). Timing ("we're in the middle of a product launch, maybe next quarter").
**The leak:** The 2--4 week sales cycle stalls at the internal sell. Priya is convinced but can't get her CEO on a call, or the CFO wants a written proposal.
**Fix:** Give Priya the internal sell toolkit: a one-page comparison, an ROI model, and a 5-minute Brand HQ demo she can show her CEO without StartSuite in the room.

### Stage 4: Onboarding (First 30 Days)
**Experience:** Week 1 is a deep strategy session. StartSuite downloads Priya's brain (and the CEO's brand opinions, and Marcus's design context). By Week 2, a Brand HQ draft exists. By Week 3, the competitive landscape is populated. By Week 4, Priya has a brand system she can hand to Marcus and say "here's your framework."
**The leak:** If the first 30 days feel like "discovery" with no tangible output, Priya's CEO will ask "what are we paying for?" Visible progress must happen weekly.
**Fix:** Deliver a visible artifact every week. Week 1: competitive landscape draft. Week 2: brand voice framework. Week 3: visual system and messaging matrix. Week 4: full Brand HQ live. Never let a week pass without something Priya can show her boss.

### Stage 5: Retention
**What keeps them paying:** The compounding context effect. Monthly strategy calls that surface competitive moves they missed. Proactive recommendations ("your top competitor just repositioned -- here's what it means"). The fact that Marcus is now 3x more productive because he has a system to work from. Content add-ons that produce work that actually sounds like Arcline.
**The leak:** If Brand HQ goes static -- if it feels like a finished product rather than a living system -- Priya will question the $3K/mo. The value must be demonstrably active every month.
**Fix:** Monthly "brand intelligence briefing" that shows what changed, what competitors did, and what StartSuite recommends. Make the intelligence feel alive.

### Stage 6: Advocacy
**What makes them refer:** When Priya's peer at another portfolio company says "I hate my agency" and Priya says "I know the feeling. Let me show you what we use." The before/after of her operational experience -- from chaos to system -- is the referral story.
**The leak:** If the transformation isn't dramatic enough to talk about, there's no referral. Incremental improvement doesn't generate word-of-mouth. The experience has to feel like a step-change.
**Fix:** Build a milestone moment into the engagement. "Before StartSuite, it took us 4 hours to produce a social post. Now it takes 45 minutes." Give Priya a stat she can quote.
