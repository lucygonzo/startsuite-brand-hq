**Archetype:** The First Marketing Hire / The Marketing Director Managing Chaos
**Core Emotional Truth:** They were hired to drive growth but spend half their time building brand infrastructure that should already exist. They need a strategic backbone, not more deliverables.

## Value Propositions

### 1. Give your marketing hire a system, not a blank canvas
You hired a VP of Marketing to drive growth. Instead, they're spending 40% of their time figuring out what the brand voice is, which logo version is current, and why slide 7 doesn't match slide 3. Brand HQ gives them a complete brand system on day one: voice guidelines, visual framework, competitive positioning, and messaging architecture. They stop building infrastructure and start building pipeline.

### 2. Everything an agency charges $100K for -- delivered in days, not months
Traditional agencies want $12K/mo, a 6-month lock-in, and 8 weeks to deliver a PDF you'll lose in Google Drive. StartSuite builds a living brand system -- strategy, competitive intelligence, voice, visual identity -- and delivers it in days. No junior handoff. No "use it or lose it" retainer. Senior strategists do the work because they are the work.

### 3. Brand intelligence that gets smarter every month
Brand HQ accumulates context. Every competitive move tracked, every strategic decision logged, every brand evolution documented. After 6 months, StartSuite knows more about your brand than a new agency could learn in their first 90 days or a new hire could absorb in their first quarter. That accumulated intelligence is why clients don't leave.

### 4. Multi-brand, multi-voice, one system
Growing companies develop sub-brands, acquire products, launch new lines. PhyCap manages Robin and Dr. Slowsar from one Brand HQ. 2047 Sports manages 165+ properties. StartSuite handles the complexity that freelancers can't and most agencies won't -- multiple brand voices, governed from a single system.

## Per-Channel Tactics

### LinkedIn

**What to post:** Content that speaks to the marketing director's operational pain. Not "brand matters" (they know). Instead: "Your brand was built at seed. Here's how to know when it's breaking."

**Tone:** Strategic, experienced, slightly empathetic. These are professionals, not scrappy founders. Speak to them as peers, not prospects.

**Frequency:** 3--4x/week from Lucy and Reagan's personal accounts. Alternate between growth-stage content and startup content.

**Example hooks:**
- "Your marketing hire quit after 6 months. It wasn't the salary. It was the brand infrastructure."
- "You have a designer but no brand system. Here's the difference and why it matters at 30 people."
- "I audited a growth company's brand last week. They had 3 taglines, 4 logo versions, and zero competitive positioning. Here's what we did in 10 days."
- "The most expensive brand decision you'll make at Series A has nothing to do with your logo."
- "Your CEO keeps asking why the brand looks inconsistent. Here's the one-page answer."

**Format:** Text-heavy posts with one supporting image (brand audit screenshot, before/after comparison, or Brand HQ demo). Carousels for "how we fixed [problem]" walkthroughs. Tag real companies where possible (with permission).

### Email

**Subject line style:** Specific to the marketing director's daily reality. Not "Transform your brand" -- instead "The brand infrastructure gap that's costing your marketing hire 15 hours/week" or "What your CEO actually means when they say 'the brand feels off.'"

**Content angle:** Problem-solution narratives drawn from real client work. One problem, one insight, one CTA. 200--300 words. Written in the voice of a peer strategist, not a sales rep.

**CTA:** "Want to see what Brand HQ looks like for a company your size?" or "I'll build a sample competitive landscape for your market in 48 hours. Free. Just reply."

**Cadence:** 1x/week for nurture. Triggered sequences after case study views, pricing page visits, or demo requests.

### Partnerships and Referral Channels

**VC portfolio introductions:** Same model as startups but positioned differently. For growth companies, the pitch to the fund partner is: "Your Series A companies are all hitting the same wall -- brand infrastructure that was built at seed and can't support their current scale. We fix that in 30 days."

**Peer marketing communities:** Exit Five, Pavilion, Superpath. Sponsor a session. Guest on a podcast episode. Contribute a case study to their newsletter. The marketing director discovers StartSuite through a trusted community, not a cold email.

**Accountant/CFO referral:** Growth companies often have a fractional CFO or accounting partner who sees the marketing spend. If that person recommends StartSuite as a cost-effective alternative to an agency, it carries weight because it comes from the budget side.

### Website

**What this person needs to see:**

1. **A dedicated "Growth Companies" section or page:** Headline: "Your brand was built for 5 people." Subline: "Brand HQ gives your growing team the strategic backbone they need."
2. **Case studies from similar companies:** Steadwell, 2047 Sports, PhyCap. With specific outcomes: time saved, brand consistency achieved, operational improvements.
3. **Comparison table:** StartSuite vs. Agency vs. Full-time hire vs. DIY. Monthly cost, capabilities included, time to value, what you own when you leave.
4. **How it works for growth companies:** Step 1: Strategy deep-dive (Week 1). Step 2: Brand HQ build (Week 2--3). Step 3: Team enablement (Week 4). Step 4: Ongoing intelligence + add-ons (Month 2+).
5. **Pricing:** Foundations at $3K/mo. Content add-ons starting at $2K/mo. No 6-month lock-in. Own everything if you leave.
6. **The internal sell toolkit:** A downloadable one-pager that the marketing director can hand to their CEO. "Here's what we get, here's what it costs, here's why it's better than hiring or agency."

**What they should NOT see:** Startup-only messaging. "Get your first logo" copy. Anything that makes it feel like StartSuite is for companies earlier than theirs. Generic agency language ("we craft bespoke experiences"). Stock photography.
