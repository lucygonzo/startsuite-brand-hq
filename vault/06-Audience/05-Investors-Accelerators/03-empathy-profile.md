**Age:** 43
**Title:** Managing Partner & Program Director, Elevation Ventures (seed-stage fund + affiliated accelerator in Tulsa, OK)
**Fund size:** $22M across 2 funds
**Portfolio:** 18 active companies (pre-seed to Series A)

## Day in the Life

**6:15 AM** -- Marcus wakes up to his phone. Three portfolio founders texted him overnight. One needs an intro to a potential customer. One is panicking about runway. One wants feedback on a pitch deck that looks like it was made in Google Slides by someone who has never seen a pitch deck before. He screenshots the deck and sighs.

**7:00 AM** -- Drops the kids at school. Listens to a VC podcast on the drive. They're talking about portfolio support as a differentiator for fund returns. He thinks about how his fund's "platform" is really just him making introductions and answering texts at midnight.

**8:30 AM** -- At the office (a co-working space in Tulsa Arts District). First call: a founder doing a practice run of their demo day pitch. The product is strong. The slides are a disaster. Different fonts on every page. The logo is blurry. The color scheme looks like a gas station rewards program. Marcus mutes himself and rubs his temples.

**9:30 AM** -- Cohort standup. Eight companies in the current accelerator batch report progress. Three of them mention they need "marketing help." What they actually mean: they need someone to make their pitch deck not look terrible, build a website that doesn't embarrass them, and figure out what to post on LinkedIn. Marcus has no systematic answer for this. He says "I'll connect you with someone" and makes a mental note he'll forget by lunch.

**10:30 AM** -- LP update call. One of his LPs asks about portfolio company progress. Marcus pulls up the portfolio tracker. Half the companies don't have updated websites. Two still have placeholder logos. One has three different company names across their LinkedIn, website, and pitch deck. The LP doesn't say anything, but Marcus feels the judgment.

**12:00 PM** -- Lunch with another fund manager. They swap notes on portfolio support. The other fund just hired a "platform designer" to help portfolio companies with pitch decks. Cost: $85K/year salary. Output: maybe 3--4 companies helped per month. Marcus does the math and grimaces.

**1:30 PM** -- Reviewing applications for the next accelerator cohort. The strongest applicants have something in common: they already look professional. Clean websites, clear positioning, consistent visual identity. The weakest applicants also have something in common: they look like they built everything in a weekend. Marcus realizes he's pattern-matching on brand quality as a proxy for founder capability, and wonders if that's fair.

**3:00 PM** -- A portfolio founder calls. She just got rejected from a partnership she was pursuing. The partner said her "brand didn't feel institutional enough." She's crushed. Marcus knows exactly what the partner meant. Her company is strong, but her brand looks like a side project. He promises to help but doesn't know how.

**4:30 PM** -- Trying to find the fund's own logo files for a conference presentation. He has 47 versions in a shared Drive folder. He texts his admin: "Which one is the current white logo?" She responds: "I think it's the one labeled 'final-v3-USETHISONE.png'?" He opens it. It's pixelated.

**6:00 PM** -- Home for dinner. His wife asks how the portfolio companies are doing. He says "They're building great products. They just all look like they were designed by engineers." She laughs. He doesn't.

**9:30 PM** -- Scrolling LinkedIn before bed. He sees a post about a startup that just rebranded. Before and after screenshots. The transformation is dramatic. He thinks: "I wish I could do this for all 18 of my companies." Then he thinks: "I wish I could do this for every cohort going forward." Then he falls asleep.

## Decision Triggers

1. **The demo day moment.** Marcus watches a portfolio company fumble a presentation because their deck looks unprofessional. The product was great. The brand killed the pitch. He decides he needs a systematic solution.
2. **The QuickBooks comparison.** Someone frames brand infrastructure as essential startup tooling -- not optional creative work. Marcus realizes: "Why don't we set this up for companies the same way we set up their accounting?"
3. **The before/after.** Marcus sees one company transform with a brand operating system. Before: amateur. After: institutional. The contrast is so stark that he immediately thinks of 5 other companies that need the same thing.
4. **The LP pressure.** An LP asks why portfolio companies "don't look more professional." Marcus realizes brand quality is reflecting on his fund's reputation.
5. **The warm intro.** Another fund manager or trusted advisor recommends StartSuite specifically. Marcus trusts peer recommendations above all other signals.
