# Tier 3: Established Businesses — Empathy Profile

**Persona:** The Marketing Director Managing Chaos
**Based On:** Aaron (247 Sports), Nicole/Dunnick archetype, FICAP contact
**Data Depth:** Deep (grounded in buyer signal mining report, multiple transcript sources)
**Last Updated:** March 26, 2026

## Who They Are

**Name for internal reference:** "Jordan" (composite persona)

Jordan is 34-42. They have 8-15 years of marketing experience. They have worked at 2-3 companies before this one and at least one agency earlier in their career. They were hired to "fix marketing" at a company that had been winging it for years. They inherited a brand that was built by someone who left, modified by three people who came after, and never documented properly.

They manage a team of 1-3 people. One is a junior designer who is good at production but cannot do strategy. One is a coordinator who handles scheduling and admin. If there is a third, it is a part-time social media person in their twenties who will leave within 18 months.

Jordan reports to a CEO, COO, or VP who does not understand marketing but has strong opinions about it. Jordan's budget is "enough to do something but not enough to do everything." They are evaluated on metrics they did not choose and cannot fully control.

They are good at their job. They are not incompetent. They are overwhelmed by a scope that would require 6-8 people to do properly, and they have 2-3.

## A Day in Jordan's Life

**6:30 AM** — Phone alarm. Before feet hit the floor, check Slack (3 messages from the CEO about a competitor's LinkedIn post), email (agency sent the wrong files again), and Instagram (the social coordinator posted something with the old logo).

**7:45 AM** — In the car. Listening to a marketing podcast. Half-listening. Thinking about the board deck that is due Friday and the brand refresh presentation that has been "in progress" for four months.

**8:15 AM** — At the desk. Open the project management tool. 47 tasks. 12 are overdue. The CEO wants a one-pager for a conference by noon. The sales team needs new collateral "yesterday." The designer is out sick. The website has a typo that a board member noticed.

**9:00 AM** — Meeting with the CEO. The CEO saw a competitor at a conference and wants to "completely rethink our positioning." Jordan knows this means a 45-minute conversation that produces no actionable outcome but creates anxiety for weeks. Jordan takes notes, nods, and will translate "rethink our positioning" into "update three slides and move on."

**10:00 AM** — Emergency. The events team is printing signage for a trade show next week and the file they have uses a logo from 2019. Jordan cannot find the current high-res logo. It takes 22 minutes of searching through Google Drive, Dropbox, and email to find it. This has happened four times this quarter.

**10:30 AM** — Vendor call with the agency. The agency presents three options for a campaign. All three feel generic. Jordan knows they would be better if the agency understood the brand more deeply, but the agency account manager changes every 6 months and the brief Jordan wrote was 2 pages because that is all they had time for. Jordan picks Option B and hates it.

**11:15 AM** — The VP of Sales walks in. "Can you make a quick brochure for this new product?" Jordan knows "quick" means 8 hours of work. Says yes anyway because saying no means being labeled "not a team player."

**12:00 PM** — Lunch at desk. Scrolling LinkedIn. Sees a post from a marketing director at a competitor who seems to have their brand together. Feels a mix of admiration and defeat. Wonders: "How do they have time for this?"

**1:00 PM** — Working on the board deck. Pulling data from four different platforms. The social media numbers look bad because nobody set up proper tracking when the platforms changed their APIs. Jordan will present the best version of the truth and hope nobody asks hard questions.

**2:30 PM** — The new hire in product marketing asks Jordan for "the brand guidelines." Jordan sends a PDF from 2022 that is partially out of date. There is no single source of truth. There are three versions in three different folders and none of them are complete.

**3:15 PM** — AI panic moment. The CEO read an article about AI in marketing and wants to know "what are we doing with AI?" Jordan has experimented with ChatGPT and Claude but cannot explain to the CEO how it connects to their brand strategy. The CEO wants results. Jordan wants guidance.

**4:00 PM** — Trying to write copy for the new product landing page. Staring at a blank screen. Knows the brand voice is supposed to be "professional but approachable" but those words mean nothing concrete. Every piece of copy Jordan writes gets edited by the CEO into something that sounds like a different company.

**5:30 PM** — Leaving. 6 of the 12 overdue tasks are still overdue. The board deck is 60% done. The brand refresh presentation has not been touched. Tomorrow will be the same.

**9:00 PM** — On the couch. Phone buzzes. The social coordinator posted a Story with the wrong brand colors. Jordan fixes it from the couch and wonders if this is what a marketing career is supposed to feel like.

## How They Find Solutions

### Discovery Triggers

1. **Breaking point moment.** A visible brand failure — wrong logo on printed materials, embarrassing inconsistency in a public-facing document, a board member asking "why does our website look different from our brochures?" This is the moment they start looking.

2. **Peer conversation.** Another marketing director mentions a tool, a service, or an approach that worked for them. Jordan trusts peers more than vendors. A single "you should talk to these people" from a respected peer outweighs 100 cold emails.

3. **Conference or event.** Jordan attends a session about brand operations or marketing efficiency and realizes there are structured solutions for what they have been doing ad hoc. The realization is not "I need an agency." It is "I need a system."

4. **New leadership pressure.** A new CEO or board member arrives and asks "what is our brand strategy?" Jordan has to produce something coherent and realizes the answer is "we don't have one, we have a collection of things we have done."

### Research Behavior

- Google search: "brand management system for mid-size companies," "alternative to creative agency," "brand operating system"
- LinkedIn: searches for people who have solved similar problems, follows relevant thought leaders, reads long-form posts about brand strategy
- Peer outreach: DMs 2-3 marketing directors they trust and asks "who do you use for brand?"
- Does NOT: browse Product Hunt, read startup-focused blogs, or respond to cold outreach from agencies

### Evaluation Criteria

Jordan evaluates solutions through the lens of "can I defend this decision?" Everything they recommend to leadership must have:

1. **Clear ROI story.** Not vague. "This replaces a $150K hire" or "this cuts our agency spend by 40%."
2. **Proof at similar scale.** Case studies from companies their size, not from startups or Fortune 500s.
3. **Low internal change management.** The CEO is already skeptical of new tools. If this requires 6 meetings to implement, it will not happen.
4. **Professional presentation.** If the vendor's own brand is sloppy, Jordan will not trust them with theirs.

## How They Decide

### Decision Process (4-8 weeks typical)

| Week | Action | What Jordan Needs |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| 1 | Discovery call with StartSuite | Understand: Is this for companies my size? Can you handle our complexity? What does it cost? |
| 2 | Internal socialization | Jordan shows Brand HQ to 1-2 trusted colleagues. Gauges reaction. Needs to see the "spark in the eye." |
| 3 | Demo for stakeholders | CEO or VP sees the demo. Asks: "What does this cost?" and "What do we get?" Jordan needs a one-pager. |
| 4 | Proposal review | Jordan builds the internal case. Compares to alternatives (in-house hire, agency, status quo). Needs ROI data. |
| 5-6 | Procurement / legal | Vendor questionnaire, security review, contract redline. Jordan needs a procurement-ready package. |
| 7-8 | Decision | Final approval from 2-3 stakeholders. Budget allocation. Start date set. |

### Decision Influencers

- **CEO/COO:** "Is this worth the money? Will it make us look more professional?"
- **CFO:** "How does this compare to what we're already spending on agencies and tools?"
- **VP Sales:** "Will this help us produce collateral faster?"
- **Jordan's direct team:** "Will this make my job easier or add more work?"

## Frustrations (Grounded in Buyer Mining Report)

### The Retainer Resentment

> "I don't like retainers." — Nicole/Dunnick (via Reagan, Board Meeting)

Jordan has been through the retainer cycle. Pay $8K/month. Use $4K of value. Get told the unused hours "don't roll over." Feel like the agency is incentivized to be slow because they get paid regardless. This resentment runs deep. Any pricing model that smells like a traditional retainer triggers immediate resistance.

**What they want instead:** Transparency. Credits that roll over. A model where they pay for value received, not time allocated. The token/credit system addresses this directly.

### The "Defend Myself" Anxiety

> "I need to be grounded and be confident enough of myself to know what I'm doing is working and be able to defend myself. I have to defend myself." — Lucy, describing the marketing hire's inner monologue

Jordan lives under scrutiny. Every marketing decision is second-guessed by someone who does not understand marketing. The CEO has opinions. The board asks questions. The sales team blames marketing when leads are down. Jordan needs ammunition — data, rationale, documented strategy — to survive internal politics.

**What they want:** Brand HQ as a defense system. A decision log that shows WHY choices were made. Competitive intelligence that proves they are paying attention. An audit trail that turns "I think" into "the data shows."

### The Context Loss Loop

> "You lost another young person in their 20s who was running socials... they're not the ones that hold all the context. And you have to do a download." — Lucy

Jordan has onboarded 3 agencies and 4 employees in the last 5 years. Each time, the institutional knowledge resets to zero. The agency learns the brand for 3 months, starts producing decent work, then the account manager leaves and someone new asks the same questions again. Employees leave and take the context with them. Jordan is the only constant, and Jordan is exhausted from being the single point of institutional memory.

**What they want:** A system that holds the context regardless of who comes and goes. Brand HQ is the institutional memory that never leaves.

### The AI Overwhelm

> "People are sitting there trying to learn it right now and then within two weeks they're like, wait, I haven't touched grass. And it isn't even working yet." — Lucy

Jordan knows AI is important. The CEO keeps asking about it. Jordan has tried ChatGPT, maybe Claude. The first 80% is exciting — then personalization hits a wall. The AI does not know their brand. It produces generic outputs that require more editing than writing from scratch would have taken. Jordan does not have time to become an AI expert AND do their job.

**What they want:** Someone to handle the AI integration for them. Not another tool to learn. A layer that makes AI work FOR their brand without requiring Jordan to become a prompt engineer.

### The Agency-Burned Overlay

This is not a frustration — it is a psychological filter that colors every interaction. Jordan has been through 2-3 agencies. The pattern is always the same:

1. **Honeymoon phase:** Agency is excited, puts senior people on the account, produces great work.
2. **Decline phase:** Senior people rotate off. Junior team takes over. Quality drops. Agency starts "phoning it in."
3. **Resentment phase:** Jordan is paying full price for half-effort work. Feels trapped because switching is expensive and time-consuming.
4. **Exit phase:** Jordan fires the agency. Internalizes the work. Gets overwhelmed. Cycle repeats.

> "She's worked with so many agencies before." — Reagan, about Nicole

**What they need to see from StartSuite:** Proof that this is different. The operating system model means the value accrues to the CLIENT, not to StartSuite. If Jordan leaves StartSuite, they keep Brand HQ. The context does not walk out the door. This is the fundamental difference, and it must be stated explicitly and repeatedly.

## Trust Signals (What Makes Jordan Believe)

1. **Brand HQ itself.** If Jordan visits Brand HQ and sees something more sophisticated than their own brand infrastructure, the capability is proven without a single word of sales copy. The product IS the proof.

2. **Named clients at similar scale.** "We work with a CBS Sports property managing 165+ programs" is immediately credible. "We work with a Luxottica brand" signals enterprise capability. These names must be visible.

3. **Professionalism of materials.** Jordan evaluates vendors by how they present themselves. If StartSuite's own website, proposals, and communications are sloppy, Jordan assumes the deliverables will be too. Every touchpoint is an audition.

4. **Speed with substance.** "We built Brand HQ for 247 Sports in 2 days" is impressive ONLY if the quality is evident. Speed without quality signals AI slop. Speed with quality signals genuine capability.

5. **The anti-agency positioning.** Explicitly saying "we are not an agency" is a trust signal for this tier. Jordan is tired of agencies. Hearing "we build systems, not campaigns" immediately differentiates.

6. **Transparency about limitations.** Jordan has been lied to by vendors before. A StartSuite rep who says "here is what we are great at and here is what we are not" earns more trust than one who promises everything.
