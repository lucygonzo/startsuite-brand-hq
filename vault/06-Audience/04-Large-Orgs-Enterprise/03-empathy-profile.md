# Tier 4: Large Organizations / Enterprise — Empathy Profile

**Persona:** The Senior Brand Leader
**Based On:** Nicole (Dunnick Companies) and Jed (Costa Sunglasses) archetypes from buyer signal mining report
**Data Depth:** Deep (multiple transcript sources, direct quotes, behavioral signals)
**Last Updated:** March 26, 2026

---

## Who They Are

**Name for internal reference:** "Morgan" (composite persona)

Morgan is 38-52. They have 15-25 years of marketing experience. They have worked at 3-5 companies, including at least one Fortune 500. They have managed teams of 10-30 people. They have overseen budgets of $2M-$20M. They have hired and fired agencies. They have led rebrands, product launches, and brand unification projects.

They chose to join a large, privately-held or family-owned company because they saw the opportunity that others miss: a company with real substance, real history, and a brand that is dramatically under-performing relative to its actual quality. Morgan looked at this company and thought: "This is a $200M+ company with a $20M brand. I can close that gap."

Morgan has a team of 3-8 people. In a company with 500-5,000+ employees across multiple divisions, this team is responsible for everything visual, verbal, digital, and strategic about how the company presents itself to the world. They are a SWAT team operating at a scope that requires a battalion.

Morgan reports to a CEO, COO, or President who hired them specifically to professionalize the brand. The executive mandate is real, but the resources have not caught up to the ambition. Morgan has budget authority but not unlimited budget. Every dollar must be justified.

Morgan has seen everything. They have managed global campaigns. They have navigated corporate politics at companies with 50,000 employees. They have survived reorgs. They are not easily impressed. They do not respond to pitches. They respond to proof.

## The Two Archetypes

### Archetype A: Nicole (The Operator Who Chose the Challenge)

Nicole left a Fortune 200 company to join a 100-year-old, family-owned construction company with 700+ employees. She did this deliberately. She saw a company where the operational excellence far exceeded the brand sophistication. She saw a century of stories that had never been told properly. She saw a marketing function that was reactive and fragmented, and she decided to be the person who fixed it.

Nicole's defining characteristics:
- **Agency-burned.** She has worked with "so many agencies." She does not want another retainer. She does not want another account manager who changes every 6 months. She does not want to pay for hours she did not use.
- **Systems-thinker.** She does not think in campaigns. She thinks in systems. She already built an audience-based messaging framework. She already uses AI tools. She wants to systematize, not just produce.
- **Sophisticated buyer.** She knows what good looks like because she has seen it at scale. She can spot a vendor who is overpromising in the first 5 minutes of a call. She values honesty over polish.
- **Internal champion who needs ammunition.** She has executive support but still needs to prove ROI. She needs tools that help her build the internal case for every investment.

### Archetype B: Jed (The Enterprise Executive Who Wants Augmentation)

Jed was CMO of Under Armour outdoor and running categories. He now leads marketing at Costa Sunglasses, owned by Luxottica (the largest eyewear company in the world). He operates at international scale. He has a team. He has infrastructure. He does not need basics.

Jed's defining characteristics:
- **Strategic, not tactical.** He does not need someone to make posts. He needs someone who can think at the level of brand positioning, competitive landscape, and market strategy.
- **Augmentation buyer.** He has a team. He wants to make that team more effective. He wants tools and intelligence that multiply what his existing people can do.
- **Quiet evaluator.** He does not express objections loudly. He watches. He evaluates. He makes decisions based on capability evidence, not sales presentations.
- **Network-driven.** He responds to people he trusts, not to companies he does not know. Reagan's personal relationship is the entire sales channel here.

## A Day in Morgan's Life

**6:00 AM** — Up before the alarm. Checking email on the phone. A board member forwarded a competitor's new brand campaign with a one-line message: "Why don't we look like this?" This will require a 3-page response that Morgan will write today even though the answer is "because our brand budget is 1/10th of theirs and my team is 1/5th the size they need."

**7:15 AM** — Driving in. Reviewing the week mentally. Monday: all-hands meeting where Morgan presents the quarterly brand update to leadership. Tuesday: agency review — the current agency's contract is up and Morgan is deciding whether to renew, replace, or eliminate. Wednesday: brand unification workshop with division leaders who each think their sub-brand should stay independent. Thursday: CEO wants to discuss "what we're doing about AI." Friday: trying to write the strategy brief that has been on the back burner for 6 weeks.

**8:00 AM** — Office. First meeting: the designer needs approval on 4 sets of materials for 4 different divisions. Each division has slightly different brand guidelines because the unification work is not finished. Morgan makes judgment calls on which inconsistencies to allow today and which to push back on. This negotiation happens every single day.

**9:30 AM** — The CEO's assistant schedules a "quick chat." Nothing with the CEO is quick. The CEO saw a LinkedIn post about AI in marketing and wants to know the plan. Morgan has a plan, but explaining it in a way the CEO will understand (and not micromanage) requires translation work. Morgan will spend 45 minutes on a conversation that could have been an email, and then 30 minutes recovering the lost time.

**10:30 AM** — Division leader from the construction arm calls. They need a "quick" brochure for a project bid due Friday. Morgan explains that "quick" means 12 hours of work across brand review, copywriting, design, compliance review, and proofing. The division leader does not understand why it takes so long. "It's just a brochure." Morgan says yes because the alternative is the division leader making it themselves in PowerPoint with the wrong logo.

**11:30 AM** — Reviewing the competitive landscape. Manually. Morgan opens competitor websites, checks their LinkedIn, reads their recent press releases. This takes an hour and produces a page of notes. Morgan knows this should be automated but does not have the tool or the bandwidth to set it up.

**12:30 PM** — Lunch at the desk. Reading an article about brand management platforms. Skimming. Skeptical. Every platform claims to solve everything. None of them solve the actual problem, which is: Morgan has institutional knowledge that exists only in their head, and no system can extract it.

**1:00 PM** — Agency review prep. Pulling together performance data from the current agency. The agency's retainer is $12K/month. Morgan calculates that roughly 60% of the retainer produced usable work. The other 40% was wasted on revisions caused by the agency not understanding the brand deeply enough, or on deliverables that were technically "what was asked for" but not "what was needed." This is the retainer resentment in action.

**2:00 PM** — The junior team member who runs social media resigns. They are leaving for a digital agency that pays $15K more. Morgan now needs to hire a replacement, onboard them, and transfer all the social media context from the departing person's head to the new person's head. This will take 3 months. In the meantime, social media quality will drop and the CEO will notice.

**3:00 PM** — Brand unification meeting prep. Three division leaders need to agree on whether to consolidate sub-brands under the parent or maintain separate identities. One division leader thinks their sub-brand has too much equity to change. Another thinks consolidation is obvious. The third does not care but will object to anything that requires effort from their team. Morgan needs to facilitate this conversation without taking sides while simultaneously advocating for the strategically correct answer.

**4:00 PM** — Finally writing the strategy brief. Gets 400 words in before being pulled into a Slack thread about a press release that went out with the wrong boilerplate.

**5:30 PM** — Leaving. The strategy brief is at 400 words. The board member response is drafted but not sent. The social media hire requisition is not started. The agency decision is unmade. Tomorrow will be the same.

**8:00 PM** — At home. Scrolling LinkedIn. Sees a company that has a brand operating system — everything organized, every decision documented, competitive intelligence at their fingertips. Thinks: "That is what I need. That is what I don't have time to build."

## How They Find Solutions

### What Triggers the Search

1. **Visible failure.** A board member, a client, or a partner sees a brand inconsistency and raises it publicly. The embarrassment creates urgency.

2. **Team attrition.** A key person leaves and Morgan realizes the entire brand system lived in that person's head. The institutional knowledge walk-out is the trigger.

3. **Agency contract renewal.** The moment when Morgan has to decide: renew the agency that is delivering 60% value, or find something better? This is a natural evaluation window.

4. **Executive mandate.** The CEO decides brand needs attention. This may be triggered by a competitor, a board member, or a strategic initiative. Morgan has air cover but needs to move fast.

5. **Peer recommendation.** Another senior marketing leader says "you should look at this." This is the single most powerful trigger. One recommendation from a trusted peer outweighs months of marketing.

### Research Behavior

Morgan does not "research" in the traditional sense. They do not Google "best brand management tools." Instead:

- They ask their network. Privately. Via text, DM, or phone call. "What are you using? Who is good?"
- They evaluate based on first-person experience. If someone offers a demo, Morgan shows up skeptical and watches for substance.
- They check references. Not the references the vendor provides — the ones they find through their own network.
- They assess the vendor's own brand. If the vendor's website, materials, and communication are not excellent, Morgan assumes the deliverables will not be either.

## How They Decide

### Decision Framework

Morgan's decision-making is not emotional. It is calculated.

| Factor | Weight | What They Look For |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| **Capability evidence** | 30% | Can they handle our complexity? Show me proof with a company at our scale. |
| **Strategic depth** | 25% | Do they think at the level I think? Or are they glorified production? |
| **Model fit** | 20% | Does the engagement model match how I want to work? (No retainers. Transparent. Flexible.) |
| **Risk mitigation** | 15% | What happens if this does not work? What is the exit plan? How much of our data is portable? |
| **Relationship quality** | 10% | Do I trust the people? Will they tell me the truth when the truth is uncomfortable? |

### Decision Timeline: 6-12 Weeks

| Week | Action | Morgan's Mindset |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | Introduction via warm connection. First call. | "Prove to me in 30 minutes that you understand companies like mine." |
| 3-4 | Second meeting. Deeper dive. See Brand HQ with real data. | "This is interesting. But can it scale? Let me pressure-test." |
| 5-6 | Internal socialization. Morgan shows Brand HQ to the CEO. | "I need the CEO to not say no. I don't need enthusiasm, I need absence of objection." |
| 7-8 | Stakeholder meetings. CFO, CTO, division leaders. | "Each person needs a different version of why this matters." |
| 9-10 | Procurement process. Legal review. | "If they can't navigate our procurement process efficiently, they can't handle us as a client." |
| 11-12 | Decision. Contract execution. | "I'm betting my professional reputation on this. It better work." |

## Frustrations (Grounded in Buyer Mining Report)

### The Retainer Resentment (CRITICAL — The Strongest Signal)

> "Her comment was, I don't like retainers... she's worked with so many agencies before." — Reagan, about Nicole

> "Then you're like, sorry you didn't use us. You still charged you for what you did because you didn't decide to use us. So use it or lose it." — Lucy, describing the retainer resentment problem

This is not mild discomfort. This is visceral. Morgan has paid $100K+ per year in agency retainers and watched 30-40% of that value evaporate into unused hours, junior staff doing senior work, and account managers who changed every 6 months. The resentment is not about price — it is about fairness. Morgan will pay premium prices for premium value. They will not pay premium prices for a model that profits from their under-utilization.

### The Context Vacuum

> "You lost another young person in their 20s who was running socials... they're not the ones that hold all the context. And you have to do a download." — Lucy

Morgan has onboarded 5-8 agencies and 10-15 employees over their career. Every time, the same painful download process. Every time, the new person or agency starts from scratch. Every time, 2-3 months of context-building before they produce anything useful. Morgan is the single thread of institutional memory, and they are exhausted by it.

### The "Big Brand, Tiny Team" Paradox

Morgan manages a brand that touches 500-5,000+ people, dozens of markets, multiple divisions, and countless external partners. They do this with 3-8 people. The math does not work. They know it does not work. Their leadership does not understand why the marketing team "can't just handle it." The gap between scope and resources is the defining frustration of their career.

### The AI Accountability Fear

> "I hit this button and this happens, but I can't explain to my boss, especially if there's like some weird jailbreak." — Lucy, about enterprise AI anxiety

Morgan is expected to "use AI" but also expected to maintain brand integrity, legal compliance, and accountability for every output. If an AI tool produces something off-brand or inaccurate and it reaches the public, Morgan is accountable. The CEO does not want to hear "the AI did it." Morgan needs a system where AI is governed, traceable, and auditable — not just fast.

### The Sub-Brand Negotiation Fatigue

> "She wants to put it all under one brand. The decision to separate different brands came from somewhere. And I'm really curious to know why they did that, because that context was lost." — Lucy, about Nicole and Dunnick

Morgan is trying to unify sub-brands across divisions. Each division leader has emotional attachment to their brand. The original reasons for separating the brands may have been valid 15 years ago but are lost to history. Morgan is navigating organizational politics while simultaneously trying to establish a coherent brand architecture. This is the hardest part of the job, and no agency has ever helped with it meaningfully.

## The "Agency-Burned" Overlay (Very Strong at This Tier)

Morgan has not just been burned once. They have been through the full agency cycle 3-5 times. The pattern is deeply ingrained:

### The Cycle Morgan Has Lived Through

1. **Pitch:** Agency brings their A-team. Senior strategists. Beautiful deck. Impressive case studies. Morgan thinks: "Maybe this time will be different."

2. **Kickoff:** First month is energizing. The agency is learning. Questions are sharp. Deliverables are on time. Senior team is present.

3. **Handoff:** Month 3-4. The senior team "transitions" the account to the "day-to-day team." This team is 5-10 years less experienced. They ask questions that were already answered in the brief.

4. **Decline:** Month 6-12. Quality drops. Revisions increase. The agency starts delivering "what was asked for" instead of "what was needed." Morgan spends more time managing the agency than the agency saves in time.

5. **Resentment:** Month 12-18. Morgan calculates the true ROI and realizes it is negative or marginal. The retainer keeps billing. The agency keeps delivering adequate work. Nobody is happy but switching is expensive.

6. **Exit:** Month 18-24. Morgan fires the agency. Brings the work in-house. Gets overwhelmed. The cycle begins again.

**What breaks the cycle:** The operating system model. The value accrues to Morgan's company, not to StartSuite. Brand HQ is the client's property. The context does not leave when the vendor relationship ends. This fundamentally changes the power dynamic — and it must be stated explicitly, early, and often.

## Trust Signals (What Makes Morgan Believe)

1. **Proof with their data.** A generic demo is ignored. A Brand HQ section built with their actual brand data — their competitive landscape, their brand inconsistencies, their market position — is impossible to ignore. This is the proof of concept that converts.

2. **Named companies at their scale.** "We work with a 700-person, 100-year-old company" and "We work with a Luxottica brand" are the two strongest credibility signals. These must be in every first conversation.

3. **Honesty about limitations.** Morgan can spot overselling in seconds. A vendor who says "here is what we are excellent at and here is what we do not do" earns more trust than one who promises everything.

4. **The anti-agency pitch, backed by structure.** It is not enough to say "we are not an agency." Show the structural difference: credits vs. retainer hours, client-owned Brand HQ vs. agency-owned intellectual property, accumulated context vs. account team rotation.

5. **Speed with substance.** Morgan has waited 6-12 months for agency deliverables. Hearing "we can have your Brand HQ running in two weeks" is remarkable — but only if the quality matches the speed.

6. **Personal credibility of the principals.** Morgan does not buy from companies. They buy from people. Lucy's strategic depth and Reagan's enterprise relationship experience are the trust foundation. The product supports the relationship, not the other way around.
