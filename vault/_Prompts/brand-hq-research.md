## 11 GLOBAL RESEARCH RULES

These apply to every section of research and every piece of output. No exceptions.

1. **No generic advice.** Every insight must be specific to this company, this industry, and this competitive landscape. If a recommendation could apply to any company, it is not good enough.
2. **Surface what others miss.** We need insights that set the client above and beyond competition. Go past the obvious. Find the pattern nobody has named.
3. **Strategy serves action.** Every recommendation must be in service of answering key questions, gathering insights, or closing leaky funnels. No strategy for strategy's sake.
4. **No em dashes.** Do not use em dashes anywhere. Do not use "--" in place of em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or restructure the sentence.
5. **Creative sentence structure.** Avoid patterns like "It's not XX. It's Y." or "This isn't about X. This is about Y." These are crutches. Find better constructions.
6. **Root everything in evidence.** Reference what stakeholders and clients have told us. Use the contact's insights, quotes, and confirmed decisions. Do not invent context.
7. **Be smart with tokens.** Use what we already have before researching externally. Only go to the web for new context, insights, or updates we lack.
8. **Parent before parts.** Research the core company first, then each sub-brand. Understand the whole before the components.
9. **Precision on brand elements.** When referencing colors or brand elements, pull specific hex codes and exact taglines. No approximations.
10. **Publicly observable data only.** Pull from websites, social profiles, job postings, press releases, regulatory filings, trade publications. Do not fabricate data points.
11. **Strategy should never exist for its own sake.** If a finding does not help the client make a better decision, close a gap, or see an opportunity, cut it. Useful beats comprehensive.

## COMPETITIVE FRAMEWORK (6 categories, proprietary)

Use these categories to structure competitive analysis. Client-facing files use the "Client Label" (not the internal name):

| # | Internal Name | Client Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Direct Competitors | Head-to-Head Market Players | Same problem, similar solution |
| 2 | Adjacent Competitors | Alternative Solution Providers | Same problem, different solution |
| 3 | Industry Relatives | Industry Peers | Same industry, not direct competitors |
| 4 | Audience Relatives | Attention Competitors | Competing for attention from same audience |
| 5 | Sister-Business Models | Structural Parallels | Different industry, similar business model |
| 6 | Inspiration Companies | Messaging & Positioning Benchmarks | Similar challenges, excellent execution |

Place every competitor in exactly one category. Justify the placement. If a competitor straddles two categories, pick the primary one and note the overlap.

## RESEARCH ORDER (non-negotiable)

Complete these in sequence. Do not skip ahead to strategy before research is done.

1. **Company Profile** (core company first, then sub-brands)
2. **Target Audience Research** (BEFORE competitive, always)
3. **Competitive Landscape** (filtered through audience needs, not just who looks similar)
4. **Digital Ecosystem** (SEO, GEO, social presence, tech stack)
5. **Visual Identity Audit** (web-rip colors, fonts, logos, design patterns)
6. **Verbal Identity Inventory** (scrape all current language across every touchpoint)
7. **Industry Landscape** (macro trends, parallel industries, timing windows)
8. **Strategy Development** (only after all research is complete)

## RESEARCH SOURCES (priority order)

Search and synthesize from the following:

1. Company website, all pages: about, product, pricing, blog, careers, press
2. Founder LinkedIn profiles and any public interviews, podcasts, or talks
3. Crunchbase and PitchBook for funding history, investors, team size
4. Press coverage: TechCrunch, industry publications, local business press
5. Product reviews: G2, Capterra, App Store, Product Hunt, Reddit, Twitter/X
6. Job listings, current and archived (these reveal strategic priorities companies do not announce publicly)
7. Social media presence: LinkedIn, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok if relevant
8. Competitor websites and positioning for landscape context
9. Industry reports or market sizing data if publicly available

For every factual claim, note the source. For every quote, note who said it, where, and when.

## SOURCE VALIDATION RULES

- **Validated research only.** Every claim must be traceable to a real source.
- **Do not infer, approximate, or fill gaps with assumptions.** If you cannot verify something, say so explicitly and flag it for human review.
- **A gap that is honestly named is more useful than a gap that is papered over.**
- **Specificity beats polish.** A rough answer with a source is more useful than a clean answer with no basis.
- **Do not clean up customer language.** The rawness is the point.
- **Separate confirmed features from roadmap items.** Do not mix what exists today with what is planned.
- **Note confidence level for each metric:** verified by company, third-party reported, or estimated by press.
- **Source check at the end of every section.** List the specific pages and sources used.

## OUTPUT FORMAT: 18 VAULT PAGES

Organize findings into the sections below. Each section maps to a vault folder. Use clear headers. Write in complete sentences, not bullet fragments. Where direct quotes exist, include them with source attribution.

### PAGE 2: COMPANY PROFILE (vault: 02-Company/)

**Files to produce:** company-overview.md, team-highlights.md, founding-story.md

- Full legal name and any operating name or brand name distinctions

### PAGE 3: BRAND IDENTITY (vault: 01-Identity/)

**Files to produce:** archetype.md, core-narrative.md, personality.md, messaging-hierarchy.md, positioning-statement.md

- Personality traits and characteristics
- Key phrases with rationale
- Messaging hierarchy
- Messaging gaps: what should be said that is not being said
- Audience perception: first impression + key questions a newcomer would have
- Competitive positioning statement

**Insight prompt:** If this company's brand were a person, who would they be? Who are they trying to be? Where is the gap between those two?

### PAGE 4: GAP ANALYSIS (vault: 14-Gap-Analysis/)

- Leaky funnel audits per audience segment: Awareness > Consideration > Decision > Adoption, with severity color-coding
- Current State > Desired State gaps with severity and action guidance
- Best-in-class benchmarks for comparison

### PAGE 5: VISUAL IDENTITY (vault: 03-Visual/)

**Files to produce:** color-palette.md, typography.md, logo-usage.md, design-principles.md

- Current logo description: shape, style, weight, distinctive features

- Design principles
- Key visual motifs or design patterns that repeat
- What is worth preserving vs. what feels dated or generic

**Insight prompt:** Is there a visual direction that would be completely unoccupied in this competitive space and also genuinely true to who this company is?

### PAGE 6: VERBAL IDENTITY (vault: 04-Verbal/)

**Files to produce:** voice-guidelines.md, messaging-matrix.md, copy-blocks.md, what-not-to-say.md, taglines.md

- Core message with audience toggle (messaging adapts per audience segment)

- Tone attributes with do/don't examples
- Copy blocks: one-liner, two-sentence, LinkedIn bio, pitch intro, investor description
- Naming conventions
- "What NOT to Say" section
- 10-15 direct quotes from founders across interviews, podcasts, LinkedIn (capture natural speech)
- 5-10 customer reviews in unfiltered language
- Language that feels authentic vs. language that sounds like everyone else (retire list)

**Insight prompt:** What is the one phrase or idea this company keeps circling back to that they have not yet built a message around explicitly?

### PAGE 7: SERVICES (vault: 05-Services/)

**Files to produce:** product-tiers.md, deliverables.md

- Product tiers with clear scope (included/not included)

- Deliverables lists per tier

**Insight prompt:** What does the product do that it has not yet figured out how to talk about clearly?

---

### PAGE 8: AUDIENCE (vault: 06-Audience/)

**Files to produce:** segmentation.md, personas.md, psychographics.md

For each meaningful segment:
- Who they are: role, company type, stage, size
- What they are trying to accomplish
- What they are afraid of or frustrated by, in their own words
- Specific language they use to describe their problem (from reviews, forums, social)
- How they discover and evaluate solutions
- Why they choose this product over alternatives
- Why they might not choose it, or where they churn
- TAM/SAM/OAM sizing
- Detailed segment profiles with scoring (revenue/strategic/productFit/acquisition)
- Personas with day-in-the-life stories
- Psychographic profiles: values, beliefs, fears, media habits
- Real client examples mapped to segments

**Insight prompt:** Is there a segment this company is clearly serving well that they are not explicitly marketing to? Is there a segment they are marketing to that the product does not actually serve well?

### PAGE 9: COMPETITIVE (vault: 07-Competitive/)

**Files to produce:** landscape.md, swot.md, positioning-matrix.md, whitespace.md

For each of the top 5-8 competitors (organized by the 6-category framework):
- Company name and website
- What they actually do, in plain language
- Primary positioning message (direct quote from homepage)
- Pricing model if public
- Strengths and known weaknesses (with customer quotes from reviews)
- Visual identity and brand feel
- Who they are built for
- How they talk about themselves vs. how customers describe them

**Landscape synthesis (required):**
- What is the dominant positioning in this category?
- What words, promises, and frames repeat across every competitor? (table stakes, not differentiators)
- Where is the whitespace? What is nobody saying that could be said and validated?
- What emotional territory is completely unclaimed?
- Who is the market leader and why specifically?
- What does a competitor appear to be doing well but is actually a long-term liability?
- What is the one insight that would genuinely surprise the client?

SWOT analysis and positioning matrix with labeled axes.

### PAGE 16: ACTION ITEMS (vault: 13-Workspace/)

### STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND TIMING (cross-cutting, feeds into 00-Overview and 09-Go-to-Market)

### GAPS AND FLAGS FOR HUMAN REVIEW (final section, for internal use only)

This section is for the human reviewer, not the client. Be direct.

- Which pages above are thin, uncertain, or based on inference rather than verified evidence? List them by page number.
- What specific questions should be answered in a discovery call or supplemental brief?
- What information is simply not publicly available and must come from the client?
- What is the single most important thing learned that should shape everything downstream?
- What is the single biggest brand or positioning opportunity visible in the landscape? Commit to an answer. Do not hedge.

## FINAL INSTRUCTIONS

Specificity beats polish. A rough answer with a source is more useful than a clean answer with no basis.

Pull direct quotes wherever possible. Cite every source.

Do not fill gaps with guesses. Name the gap, explain why it matters, and flag it for the human reviewer.

The gaps section is not an afterthought. It is part of the deliverable.

Format the output so it can be dropped directly into the vault folder structure. Use the page headers and file names exactly as written above.

The goal is not a report. The goal is a foundation a brand strategist can build from without starting over.
